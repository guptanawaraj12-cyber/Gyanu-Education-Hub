Class 8 Science

Cell - Structure and Functions

Learn about the basic unit of life - the cell. Understand cell structure, organelles, and their functions.

Key Points

  • Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.
  • Discovery of Cell: Discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665.
  • Cell Theory: All living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and new cells arise from existing cells.
  • Prokaryotic Cell: No well-defined nucleus (e.g., Bacteria).
  • Eukaryotic Cell: Has a well-defined nucleus (e.g., Plant and Animal cells).
  • Cell Organelles: Nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, etc.
  • Plant Cell vs Animal Cell: Plant cells have cell wall, chloroplasts, and large vacuoles.

Introduction

The cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms, from the smallest bacteria to the largest whale, are made up of cells. Some organisms like Amoeba are made of a single cell (unicellular), while others like humans are made of many cells (multicellular).

In this chapter, we will explore the structure of cells, the different organelles inside them, and how they function to keep living organisms alive.

1. Discovery of the Cell

The cell was first discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665. He observed thin slices of cork under a self-made microscope and saw tiny compartments that looked like "little rooms". He named them "cells". Later, Anton van Leeuwenhoek discovered living cells in pond water using a more advanced microscope.

💡 Important Note:

Robert Hooke observed dead cells (cork cells), while Leeuwenhoek was the first to observe living cells.

2. Cell Theory

The Cell Theory was proposed by Schleiden (1838) and Schwann (1839). Later, Virchow (1855) added the third point. The main points of the Cell Theory are:

  • All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  • The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.
  • All new cells arise from pre-existing cells.

3. Types of Cells

Based on the presence of a well-defined nucleus, cells are classified into two types:

3.1 Prokaryotic Cells

  • No well-defined nucleus (nuclear material is not enclosed by a membrane).
  • Membrane-bound organelles are absent.
  • Examples: Bacteria, Blue-green algae.

3.2 Eukaryotic Cells

  • Have a well-defined nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane.
  • Membrane-bound organelles are present.
  • Examples: Plant cells, Animal cells, Fungal cells.
📝 Example:

Bacteria are prokaryotic, while humans, plants, and animals are eukaryotic.

4. Cell Structure and Organelles

A cell contains various organelles that perform specific functions. Here are the main organelles:

4.1 Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

  • Outer boundary of the cell.
  • Controls the movement of substances in and out of the cell (selectively permeable).
  • Made up of lipids and proteins.

4.2 Nucleus

  • The "brain" or control center of the cell.
  • Contains DNA (genetic material).
  • Controls all cell activities.
  • Has a nuclear membrane, nucleolus, and chromatin fibers.

4.3 Cytoplasm

  • Jelly-like substance between the cell membrane and nucleus.
  • Contains water, salts, and various organelles.
  • Site for many chemical reactions.

4.4 Mitochondria

  • Known as the "Powerhouse of the cell".
  • Produces energy (ATP) through cellular respiration.
  • Has a double membrane.

4.5 Ribosomes

  • Site of protein synthesis.
  • Made of RNA and proteins.

4.6 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

  • Network of membranes for transport.
  • Rough ER (with ribosomes) - protein synthesis.
  • Smooth ER (without ribosomes) - lipid synthesis.

4.7 Golgi Apparatus

  • Packages and transports materials within the cell.
  • Modifies proteins and lipids.

4.8 Lysosomes

  • Known as the "Suicide bags of the cell".
  • Contains digestive enzymes to break down waste.

4.9 Vacuoles

  • Store water, waste, and other materials.
  • Plant cells have a large central vacuole.

4.10 Chloroplasts (Only in Plant Cells)

  • Site of photosynthesis.
  • Contains chlorophyll (green pigment).
  • Converts light energy into chemical energy.

4.11 Cell Wall (Only in Plant Cells)

  • Outer rigid layer outside the cell membrane.
  • Provides structural support and protection.
  • Made of cellulose.

5. Difference between Plant Cell and Animal Cell

FeaturePlant CellAnimal Cell
Cell WallPresent (made of cellulose)Absent
ShapeFixed, rectangularIrregular, round
ChloroplastsPresentAbsent
VacuolesLarge central vacuoleSmall, temporary vacuoles
LysosomesRarePresent
NucleusPresent, at the peripheryPresent, in the center

6. Functions of a Cell

  • Structural Support: Cells provide structure to the body.
  • Transport: Movement of substances across the cell membrane.
  • Energy Production: Mitochondria produce energy.
  • Protein Synthesis: Ribosomes make proteins.
  • Reproduction: Cells divide to produce new cells.
  • Waste Removal: Lysosomes digest waste materials.

Chapter Summary

  • The cell is the basic unit of life, discovered by Robert Hooke.
  • Cell theory states that all living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and new cells arise from existing cells.
  • Cells are of two types: Prokaryotic (no nucleus) and Eukaryotic (with nucleus).
  • Eukaryotic cells contain organelles like nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi apparatus, etc.
  • Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts, and large vacuoles; animal cells do not.
  • Each organelle performs a specific function to keep the cell alive.

Practice Questions

Multiple Choice Questions

1. Who discovered the cell?

  • A) Anton van Leeuwenhoek
  • B) Robert Hooke
  • C) Schleiden
  • D) Virchow

2. Which organelle is known as the "Powerhouse of the cell"?

  • A) Nucleus
  • B) Ribosome
  • C) Mitochondria
  • D) Golgi apparatus

3. Which of the following is present in plant cells but not in animal cells?

  • A) Nucleus
  • B) Cell wall
  • C) Mitochondria
  • D) Ribosomes

4. The jelly-like substance inside the cell is called:

  • A) Nucleoplasm
  • B) Cytoplasm
  • C) Cell sap
  • D) Protoplasm

5. Which organelle is responsible for protein synthesis?

  • A) Ribosomes
  • B) Lysosomes
  • C) Vacuoles
  • D) Nucleus

Short Answer Questions

1. What is a cell? Why is it called the basic unit of life?

2. State the three main points of the Cell Theory.

3. Differentiate between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.

4. Write the functions of the following organelles: (a) Nucleus (b) Mitochondria (c) Ribosomes (d) Golgi apparatus

5. List any four differences between plant cells and animal cells.

Long Answer Questions

1. Draw a well-labeled diagram of a typical plant cell and explain the functions of each organelle.

2. Explain the structure and function of the nucleus. Why is it called the control center of the cell?

3. Describe the differences between plant cells and animal cells with a suitable diagram.
