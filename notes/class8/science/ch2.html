Class 8 Science (Biology)

Chapter 2: Microorganisms - Friend and Foe

Microorganisms are tiny living organisms that cannot be seen with the naked eye. They are everywhere - in air, water, soil, and even inside our bodies. Some microorganisms are beneficial (friends), while others cause diseases (foes). In this chapter, we will learn about different types of microorganisms, their uses, and how to prevent diseases caused by them.

Key Points

  • 🦠 Microorganisms (Microbes): Tiny living organisms not visible to the naked eye. Visible only under a microscope.
  • 🔬 Types of Microorganisms: Bacteria, Viruses, Fungi, Protozoa, and Algae.
  • 😊 Friendly Microbes: Used in making curd, bread, antibiotics, vaccines, and increasing soil fertility.
  • 😷 Harmful Microbes: Cause diseases like cholera, typhoid, tuberculosis, COVID-19, and common cold.
  • 🦟 Disease Transmission: Through air, water, food, insects, and physical contact.
  • 🧪 Louis Pasteur: Discovered pasteurization and proved the germ theory of disease.
  • 💊 Alexander Fleming: Discovered Penicillin (first antibiotic).
  • 💉 Edward Jenner: Developed the first vaccine (smallpox).
  • 🥛 Food Preservation: Methods include pasteurization, boiling, drying, salting, pickling, and refrigeration.
  • 🌱 Nitrogen Fixation: Bacteria like Rhizobium convert atmospheric nitrogen into usable form for plants.

1. Introduction to Microorganisms

Microorganisms (also called microbes) are living organisms that are so small that they cannot be seen with the naked eye. They can only be observed under a microscope. The word "micro" means very small, and "organism" means a living thing.

Microorganisms are found everywhere - in air, water, soil, on our skin, inside our bodies, and even in extreme environments like hot springs and deep oceans. They are the oldest form of life on Earth, existing for about 3.5 billion years!

💡 Interesting Fact:

There are more microorganisms in a handful of soil than the number of people on Earth! Your body contains about 100 trillion microbes - more than human cells.

2. Types of Microorganisms

Microorganisms are classified into five major groups:

2.1 Bacteria (Kingdom: Monera)

  • Structure: Single-celled, prokaryotic (no nucleus).
  • Size: 0.5 - 5 micrometers.
  • Shapes: Cocci (spherical), Bacilli (rod-shaped), Spirilla (spiral), Vibrio (comma-shaped).
  • Examples: E. coli (in intestines), Lactobacillus (in curd), Rhizobium (in soil), Streptococcus (causes sore throat).
  • Habitats: Soil, water, air, inside living organisms, extreme environments.

2.2 Viruses

  • Structure: Non-living outside host, living inside host. Made of DNA/RNA covered by protein coat.
  • Size: 0.02 - 0.3 micrometers (smallest of all microbes).
  • Characteristics: Can only reproduce inside a host cell. Considered borderline between living and non-living.
  • Examples: COVID-19 virus, Influenza virus (flu), HIV (AIDS), Common cold virus, Polio virus, Dengue virus.
  • Diseases caused: Common cold, flu, COVID-19, AIDS, polio, chickenpox, measles, hepatitis.

2.3 Fungi

  • Structure: Multicellular (except yeast which is unicellular), eukaryotic.
  • Characteristics: Cannot make their own food (heterotrophs). Grow as thread-like structures called hyphae.
  • Examples: Yeast (used in baking), Mushroom (edible fungus), Penicillium (produces antibiotic), Aspergillus, Rhizopus (bread mold).
  • Uses: Bread making, antibiotic production, cheese making, decomposition of dead matter.

2.4 Protozoa

  • Structure: Single-celled, eukaryotic, heterotrophic.
  • Movement: Move using flagella, cilia, or pseudopodia.
  • Examples: Amoeba (moves by pseudopodia), Paramecium (moves by cilia), Euglena (has flagella), Plasmodium (causes malaria).
  • Diseases caused: Malaria (Plasmodium), Dysentery (Entamoeba), Sleeping sickness (Trypanosoma).

2.5 Algae

  • Structure: Mostly unicellular, some multicellular, autotrophic (contain chlorophyll).
  • Characteristics: Can perform photosynthesis. Found in water bodies.
  • Examples: Chlamydomonas (unicellular), Spirogyra (filamentous), Ulva (sea lettuce).
  • Uses: Produce oxygen, food for aquatic animals, used as fertilizer, source of biofuel.

3. Useful Microorganisms (Our Friends)

Not all microorganisms are harmful. Many are extremely useful and essential for life on Earth.

3.1 In Food and Beverage Industry

  • Curd and Yogurt: Lactobacillus bacteria convert milk into curd by producing lactic acid.
  • Bread and Cakes: Yeast (fungus) produces carbon dioxide gas which makes dough rise.
  • Cheese: Different bacteria and fungi are used to make various types of cheese.
  • Idli and Dosa: Fermentation by bacteria gives these dishes their sour taste and soft texture.
  • Alcoholic Beverages: Yeast converts sugar into alcohol through fermentation.
  • Vinegar: Acetic acid bacteria convert alcohol into vinegar.
  • Pickles: Lactic acid bacteria preserve pickles and give sour taste.
📝 Example:

Have you noticed why dough rises when you add yeast? The yeast produces carbon dioxide gas which creates bubbles, making the dough fluffy and soft.

3.2 In Medicine

  • Antibiotics: Chemicals produced by microorganisms that kill or stop the growth of other harmful microbes.
    • Penicillin: Discovered by Alexander Fleming (1928) from the fungus Penicillium notatum.
    • Streptomycin: Used to treat tuberculosis.
    • Tetracycline: Used for bacterial infections.
  • Vaccines: Dead or weakened microorganisms that help build immunity against diseases.
    • Edward Jenner developed the first vaccine for smallpox (1796).
    • Louis Pasteur developed vaccines for rabies and anthrax.
    • Common vaccines: BCG (tuberculosis), Polio vaccine, COVID-19 vaccine, Hepatitis B vaccine.
  • Insulin: Genetically modified bacteria produce human insulin for diabetes patients.

3.3 In Agriculture

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Rhizobium bacteria live in root nodules of leguminous plants (peas, beans, grams) and convert atmospheric nitrogen into a form that plants can use.
  • Decomposition: Bacteria and fungi decompose dead plants and animals, returning nutrients to the soil.
  • Composting: Microorganisms break down organic waste into nutrient-rich compost.
  • Biofertilizers: Some microorganisms increase soil fertility naturally.
  • Blue-Green Algae (Cyanobacteria): Also fix nitrogen in paddy fields.

3.4 In Environmental Cleaning

  • Bioremediation: Microorganisms are used to clean oil spills and toxic waste.
  • Sewage Treatment: Bacteria break down organic waste in sewage treatment plants.
  • Decomposition: Microorganisms recycle dead matter, preventing accumulation of waste.

3.5 In Industry

  • Production of Alcohol: Yeast fermentation produces ethanol used in industries.
  • Production of Organic Acids: Citric acid, lactic acid, acetic acid are produced by microbes.
  • Production of Enzymes: Many industrial enzymes are produced by microorganisms.
  • Leather Industry: Bacteria help in curing animal hides.
💡 Important Scientists:
  • Louis Pasteur: Father of Microbiology - discovered pasteurization, proved germ theory.
  • Alexander Fleming: Discovered Penicillin (first antibiotic).
  • Edward Jenner: Developed first vaccine (smallpox).
  • Robert Koch: Discovered bacteria causing tuberculosis and cholera.

Some microorganisms cause diseases in humans, animals, and plants. They are called pathogens.

4.2 Diseases Caused by Microorganisms in Animals

  • Anthrax: Caused by Bacillus anthracis (bacteria) - affects cattle, sheep, goats.
  • Foot and Mouth Disease: Caused by virus - affects cattle, spreads through contact.
  • Rabies: Caused by rabies virus - transmitted through animal bites.
  • Bird Flu (Avian Influenza): Caused by influenza virus - affects poultry birds.

4.3 Diseases Caused by Microorganisms in Plants

  • Citrus Canker: Caused by bacteria - affects citrus fruits (orange, lemon).
  • Rust of Wheat: Caused by fungus - affects wheat crops.
  • Blight of Rice: Caused by bacteria - destroys rice crops.
  • Powdery Mildew: Caused by fungus - affects many plants.
  • Wilt of Tomato: Caused by fungus - affects tomato plants.

4.4 Food Spoilage by Microorganisms

  • Bacteria and fungi grow on food, causing it to spoil, smell bad, and become unsafe to eat.
  • Bread mold: Rhizopus grows on bread, producing black spots.
  • Fruit rot: Fungi and bacteria cause fruits and vegetables to rot.
  • Milk souring: Bacteria convert lactose into lactic acid, making milk sour.
  • Food poisoning: Caused by bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli in contaminated food.
⚠️ Prevention Tips:
  • Wash hands before eating
  • Keep food covered
  • Refrigerate perishable food
  • Drink clean boiled water
  • Get vaccinated regularly

5. Food Preservation

Food preservation is the process of treating and handling food to prevent spoilage by microorganisms and to extend its shelf life.

5.1 Methods of Food Preservation

1. Heat Treatment (Pasteurization)

  • Discovered by: Louis Pasteur
  • Process: Milk is heated to about 70°C for 15-30 seconds, then quickly cooled.
  • Purpose: Kills harmful bacteria without affecting taste.
  • Examples: Milk, fruit juices, beer, wine.

2. Boiling

  • Kills most microorganisms.
  • Common method for water purification at home.
  • Used for preserving liquid foods.

3. Refrigeration and Freezing

  • Low temperatures slow down the growth of microorganisms.
  • Refrigeration (0-4°C): For short-term preservation (vegetables, fruits, milk).
  • Freezing (-18°C): For long-term preservation (meat, fish, ice cream).

4. Drying (Dehydration)

  • Removing water prevents microorganism growth (microbes need water to grow).
  • Examples: Dried fruits (raisins, dates), dried fish, spices, grains, pulses.

5. Salting

  • Salt draws out water from food, preventing microbial growth.
  • Examples: Pickles, salted fish, salted meat, cheese.

6. Sugar Preservation

  • High sugar concentration draws water out of microbes.
  • Examples: Jams, jellies, marmalades, candied fruits, sweetened condensed milk.

7. Pickling (Acid Preservation)

  • Vinegar (acetic acid) or lemon juice creates an acidic environment that kills microbes.
  • Examples: Mango pickle, lemon pickle, mixed vegetable pickle.

8. Use of Chemical Preservatives

  • Sodium benzoate: Used in jams, squashes, soft drinks.
  • Sodium metabisulfite: Used in fruit juices, wine.
  • Nitrates and Nitrites: Used in processed meats (bacon, sausages).

9. Canning and Bottling

  • Food is sealed in airtight containers and heated to kill microbes.
  • Examples: Canned fruits, canned vegetables, canned fish, bottled sauces.

10. Vacuum Packing

  • Removing air (oxygen) prevents growth of aerobic bacteria.
  • Examples: Packed nuts, coffee, cheese, meat products.
📝 Home Preservation Examples:

At home, we commonly use: boiling (water), refrigeration (leftover food), drying (spices), salting (pickles), and sugar (jams).

6. Nitrogen Cycle

The nitrogen cycle is the process by which nitrogen is converted between its various forms in the environment. Microorganisms play a crucial role in this cycle.

Steps of Nitrogen Cycle:

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) into ammonia (NH₃) by:
    • Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules of leguminous plants
    • Azotobacter and Clostridium bacteria in soil
    • Cyanobacteria (Blue-green algae) in water and soil
    • Lightning also fixes nitrogen naturally
  • Nitrification: Conversion of ammonia into nitrites (NO₂⁻) and then into nitrates (NO₃⁻) by Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter bacteria.
  • Assimilation: Plants absorb nitrates from soil and convert them into plant proteins.
  • Ammonification: Decomposition of dead plants and animals by bacteria and fungi, releasing ammonia back into soil.
  • Denitrification: Conversion of nitrates back into atmospheric nitrogen by Pseudomonas bacteria.
💡 Important:

Plants cannot use atmospheric nitrogen directly. They need nitrogen in the form of nitrates or ammonia. That's why nitrogen-fixing bacteria are essential for plant growth.

Chapter Summary

  • Microorganisms are tiny living things visible only under a microscope.
  • 5 Types: Bacteria, Viruses, Fungi, Protozoa, Algae.
  • Useful Microbes (Friends): Make curd, bread, antibiotics, vaccines, fix nitrogen, decompose waste.
  • Harmful Microbes (Foes): Cause diseases like TB, cholera, malaria, COVID-19, food poisoning.
  • Antibiotics: Kill bacteria (Penicillin discovered by Alexander Fleming).
  • Vaccines: Build immunity against diseases (First vaccine by Edward Jenner).
  • Pasteurization: Heat treatment to kill microbes in milk (Louis Pasteur).
  • Food Preservation Methods: Boiling, refrigeration, drying, salting, pickling, canning.
  • Nitrogen Cycle: Microorganisms help fix nitrogen for plants.
  • Disease Transmission: Through air, water, food, insects, contact.
