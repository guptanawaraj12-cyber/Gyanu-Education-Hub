Chapter 3: Synthetic Fibres and Plastics

We wear clothes made of different types of fabrics. Some fabrics are obtained from natural sources like plants and animals, while others are made artificially in factories. In this chapter, we will learn about synthetic fibres, their properties, uses, and plastics - their types, uses, and environmental impact.

Key Points

  • 🧵 Fibres: Thin, thread-like structures used for making cloth, fabrics, ropes, etc.
  • 🌿 Natural Fibres: Obtained from plants (cotton, jute) and animals (wool, silk).
  • 🏭 Synthetic Fibres: Man-made fibres prepared using chemicals. Examples: Rayon, Nylon, Polyester, Acrylic.
  • 💧 Rayon: First synthetic fibre (artificial silk). Obtained from wood pulp (cellulose).
  • 💪 Nylon: Strongest synthetic fibre. Used for making ropes, stockings, parachutes.
  • 📦 Polyester: Wrinkle-resistant and easy to wash. Used for making shirts, trousers, bottles.
  • 🧥 Acrylic: Artificial wool. Used for making sweaters, blankets, shawls.
  • 🔥 Characteristics of Synthetic Fibres: Strong, durable, elastic, lightweight, quick-drying, wrinkle-resistant, and do not shrink.
  • 🧴 Plastics: Synthetic polymers that can be moulded into different shapes.
  • 🔧 Thermoplastics: Plastics that can be remoulded on heating (e.g., PVC, Polythene).
  • 🔥 Thermosetting Plastics: Plastics that cannot be remoulded once set (e.g., Bakelite, Melamine).
  • 🌍 Biodegradable: Materials that get decomposed by microorganisms (e.g., paper, cotton, wood).
  • 🚫 Non-Biodegradable: Materials that do not decompose easily (e.g., plastics, glass, metals).
  • ♻️ 3R Principle: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle - to manage plastic waste.

1. Introduction

We wear different types of clothes every day - cotton shirts, woollen sweaters, silk sarees, polyester trousers, etc. Have you ever wondered what these clothes are made of? They are made from fibres.

A fibre is a thin, thread-like structure that can be spun into yarn and then woven into fabric. Fibres can be classified into two main types:

  • Natural Fibres: Obtained from plants and animals.
  • Synthetic Fibres: Man-made fibres produced in factories using chemicals.
📝 Examples of Natural Fibres:
  • Plant fibres: Cotton, Jute, Coir, Flax, Hemp
  • Animal fibres: Wool (from sheep), Silk (from silkworm)

3. Synthetic Fibres

Synthetic fibres are man-made fibres produced by joining small chemical units called monomers to form large chain-like molecules called polymers (polymerization).

3.1 Rayon

  • Also called: Artificial Silk
  • Source: Obtained from wood pulp (cellulose) - a natural material
  • Discovery: First synthetic fibre
  • Properties:
    • Feels like silk
    • Absorbs water well
    • Breathable and comfortable
  • Uses:
    • Making clothes (shirts, sarees, dresses)
    • Bed sheets and curtains
    • Upholstery and carpets
    • Bandages and surgical products
    • Tyre cords

3.2 Nylon

  • Discovery: First fully synthetic fibre (made without any natural raw material)
  • Properties:
    • Very strong and elastic
    • Lightweight and durable
    • Resists wrinkles and shrinkage
    • Quick-drying
    • Does not absorb water easily
  • Uses:
    • Making stockings and socks
    • Ropes and fishing nets
    • Parachutes and tents
    • Toothbrush bristles
    • Car seat belts and airbags
    • Climbing ropes and safety harnesses
💡 Interesting Fact:

A nylon rope is stronger than a steel wire of the same thickness! This is why nylon is used for parachutes and climbing ropes.

3.3 Polyester

  • Properties:
    • Wrinkle-resistant (does not require ironing)
    • Easy to wash and quick-drying
    • Strong and durable
    • Retains its shape
  • Types:
    • PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate): Used for making bottles, jars, films
    • PES: Used for making fabrics
  • Uses:
    • Making shirts, trousers, jackets
    • PET bottles for soft drinks and water
    • Fleece jackets (terylene)
    • Curtains and bed sheets
    • Ropes and conveyor belts

3.4 Acrylic

  • Also called: Artificial wool
  • Properties:
    • Soft and warm like wool
    • Lightweight
    • Less expensive than natural wool
    • Does not shrink
    • Resistant to moths and insects
  • Uses:
    • Making sweaters, shawls, blankets
    • Socks and gloves
    • Stuffed toys
    • Carpets and upholstery
    • Fleece jackets

4. Characteristics of Synthetic Fibres

  • Strength: Synthetic fibres are very strong and durable.
  • Elasticity: They have good elasticity (can be stretched and return to original shape).
  • Water Absorption: They absorb very little water (quick-drying).
  • Wrinkle Resistance: They do not wrinkle easily.
  • Shrinkage: They do not shrink when washed.
  • Moth Resistance: They are not damaged by moths or insects.
  • Chemical Resistance: They are resistant to many chemicals.
  • Heat Sensitivity: They melt on heating (can be dangerous near fire).
  • Cost: They are generally cheaper than natural fibres.
⚠️ Caution:

Synthetic fibres melt on heating. Therefore, you should not wear synthetic clothes while working in the kitchen or near fire as they can catch fire easily and stick to your body.

5. Plastics

Plastics are also synthetic polymers just like synthetic fibres. They can be moulded into different shapes when heated.

5.1 Types of Plastics

A. Thermoplastics

  • Plastics that can be easily softened and remoulded on heating.
  • They can be recycled and reused.
  • Examples:
    • Polythene (used for carry bags)
    • PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride - used for pipes, wires)
    • Polypropylene (used for containers, bottles)
    • Polystyrene (used for disposable cups, packaging)

B. Thermosetting Plastics

  • Plastics that once set into a shape, cannot be remoulded by heating.
  • They do not soften on heating (they get charred).
  • Examples:
    • Bakelite (used for electrical switches, plugs, handles)
    • Melamine (used for kitchenware, floor tiles)

5.2 Properties of Plastics

  • Lightweight: Plastics are very light.
  • Strong: Some plastics are very strong.
  • Water-resistant: Plastics do not absorb water.
  • Poor conductors: Plastics are poor conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Corrosion-resistant: They do not rust or corrode.
  • Non-biodegradable: Most plastics do not decompose easily.

5.3 Uses of Plastics

  • Household items: Buckets, mugs, containers, toothbrushes, combs
  • Packaging: Carry bags, bottles, wrappers, food containers
  • Electrical items: Switches, plugs, sockets, wires covering
  • Medical field: Syringes, gloves, blood bags, tubes
  • Construction: Pipes, flooring, window frames
  • Automobiles: Dashboards, bumpers, seat covers
  • Toys and sports equipment: Toys, helmets, goggles
  • Furniture: Chairs, tables, shelves
📝 Special Plastic - Melamine:

Melamine is a thermosetting plastic that is resistant to fire and heat. It can tolerate high temperatures without catching fire. That's why it is used for making kitchenware (cooking utensils) and fireproof fabrics.

6. Biodegradable and Non-Biodegradable Materials

6.1 Biodegradable Materials

  • Materials that get decomposed by microorganisms (bacteria and fungi) in nature.
  • They break down into simple, harmless substances.
  • Examples: Paper, cotton, jute, wool, silk, wood, leather, food waste, leaves.

6.2 Non-Biodegradable Materials

  • Materials that do not get decomposed by microorganisms easily.
  • They remain in the environment for hundreds of years.
  • Examples: Plastics, glass, metals, polythene bags, synthetic fibres, rubber, electronic waste.
⚠️ Environmental Concern:

Plastics are non-biodegradable. They take 400-500 years to decompose! This causes serious environmental problems like land pollution, water pollution, and harm to animals.

7. Environmental Impact of Plastics

Problems Caused by Plastics:

  • Land Pollution: Plastic waste accumulates in landfills and does not decompose.
  • Water Pollution: Plastics clog drains and pollute rivers, lakes, and oceans.
  • Harm to Animals: Animals eat plastic bags mistaking them for food, which can cause death.
  • Soil Infertility: Plastic bags prevent water and air from reaching the soil.
  • Health Hazards: Burning plastics releases toxic gases that cause respiratory problems.
  • Marine Life: Sea animals get entangled in plastic nets and bags.
  • Microplastics: Tiny plastic particles enter our food chain through water and soil.

Prevention and Control:

  • Use cloth or jute bags instead of plastic bags.
  • Avoid using single-use plastics.
  • Segregate plastic waste for recycling.
  • Practice the 3R Principle - Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.
  • Do not litter plastic waste.
  • Use eco-friendly alternatives.
💡 The 3R Principle:
  • Reduce: Use fewer plastic products.
  • Reuse: Use plastic items multiple times.
  • Recycle: Convert used plastic into new products.

Chapter Summary

  • Fibres are thin, thread-like structures used to make fabrics.
  • Natural fibres come from plants (cotton, jute) and animals (wool, silk).
  • Synthetic fibres are man-made using chemicals - Rayon, Nylon, Polyester, Acrylic.
  • Rayon is artificial silk made from wood pulp.
  • Nylon is the first fully synthetic fibre and is very strong.
  • Polyester is wrinkle-resistant and quick-drying.
  • Acrylic is artificial wool - soft and warm.
  • Synthetic fibres are strong, durable, quick-drying, and do not shrink.
  • Plastics are synthetic polymers that can be moulded into shapes.
  • Thermoplastics can be remoulded (Polythene, PVC).
  • Thermosetting plastics cannot be remoulded (Bakelite, Melamine).
  • Biodegradable materials decompose naturally (paper, cotton).
  • Non-biodegradable materials do not decompose (plastics, glass).
  • Plastics cause serious environmental problems as they are non-biodegradable.
  • We should follow the 3R Principle - Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.
