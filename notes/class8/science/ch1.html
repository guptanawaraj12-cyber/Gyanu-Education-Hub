Chapter 1: Cell - Structure and Functions

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. In this chapter, we will learn about the discovery of cells, cell theory, types of cells, cell organelles, and the differences between plant and animal cells.

Key Points

  • 📌 Cell: The basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.
  • 🔬 Discovery: Cell was discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665.
  • 📖 Cell Theory: Proposed by Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow. All living things are made of cells.
  • 🦠 Types of Cells: Prokaryotic (no nucleus) and Eukaryotic (with nucleus).
  • 🧬 Cell Organelles: Nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi apparatus, etc.
  • 🌱 Plant Cell vs Animal Cell: Plant cells have cell wall, chloroplasts, and large vacuoles.
  • ⚡ Mitochondria: Known as the "Powerhouse of the cell" - produces energy.
  • 🧠 Nucleus: Control center of the cell - contains DNA.

1. Introduction to Cell

The cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms, from the smallest bacteria to the largest whale, are made up of cells. Some organisms like Amoeba are made of a single cell (unicellular), while others like humans are made of many cells (multicellular).

In this chapter, we will explore the structure of cells, the different organelles inside them, and how they function to keep living organisms alive.

💡 Important Note:

The word "cell" comes from the Latin word "cella" meaning "small room". Robert Hooke used this term because the cork cells looked like small rooms under his microscope.

2. Discovery of the Cell

The cell was first discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665. He observed thin slices of cork under a self-made microscope and saw tiny compartments that looked like "little rooms". He named them "cells".

Later, Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674) discovered living cells in pond water using a more advanced microscope. He was the first to observe bacteria and protozoa.

📝 Key Scientists:
  • Robert Hooke (1665) - Discovered cells (dead cork cells)
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674) - First to observe living cells
  • Robert Brown (1831) - Discovered the nucleus
  • Schleiden & Schwann (1838-39) - Proposed Cell Theory
  • Virchow (1855) - Added "Omnis cellula e cellula" (all cells arise from pre-existing cells)

3. Cell Theory

The Cell Theory was proposed by Matthias Schleiden (1838) and Theodor Schwann (1839). Later, Rudolf Virchow (1855) added the third point.

Main Points of Cell Theory:

  • Point 1: All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  • Point 2: The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.
  • Point 3: All new cells arise from pre-existing cells (Omnis cellula e cellula).
💡 Remember:

Viruses are considered non-living because they are not made of cells and cannot reproduce on their own.

4. Types of Cells

Based on the presence of a well-defined nucleus, cells are classified into two types:

4.1 Prokaryotic Cells

  • No well-defined nucleus (nuclear material is not enclosed by a membrane).
  • Membrane-bound organelles are absent.
  • They are generally smaller in size (0.5-5 µm).
  • Examples: Bacteria, Blue-green algae (Cyanobacteria), Mycoplasma.

4.2 Eukaryotic Cells

  • Have a well-defined nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane.
  • Membrane-bound organelles are present (mitochondria, ER, Golgi, etc.).
  • They are generally larger in size (10-100 µm).
  • Examples: Plant cells, Animal cells, Fungal cells, Protists.

5. Cell Structure and Organelles

A cell contains various organelles that perform specific functions. Here are the main organelles:

5.1 Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

  • Outer boundary of the cell.
  • Selectively permeable - controls movement of substances in and out.
  • Made up of lipids (phospholipids) and proteins.
  • Also called the "gatekeeper" of the cell.

5.2 Cell Wall (Only in Plant Cells)

  • Outer rigid layer outside the cell membrane.
  • Provides structural support and protection.
  • Made of cellulose (in plants), chitin (in fungi).
  • Gives plant cells their fixed shape.

5.3 Nucleus

  • The "brain" or control center of the cell.
  • Contains DNA (genetic material).
  • Controls all cell activities including growth and reproduction.
  • Has a nuclear membrane, nucleolus, and chromatin fibers.

5.4 Cytoplasm

  • Jelly-like substance between the cell membrane and nucleus.
  • Contains water, salts, and various organelles.
  • Site for many chemical reactions.

5.5 Mitochondria

  • Known as the "Powerhouse of the cell".
  • Produces energy (ATP) through cellular respiration.
  • Has a double membrane (inner membrane folded into cristae).
  • Contains its own DNA (semi-autonomous organelle).

5.6 Ribosomes

  • Site of protein synthesis.
  • Made of RNA and proteins.
  • Can be free in cytoplasm or attached to ER.

5.7 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

  • Network of membranes for transport.
  • Rough ER (RER): Has ribosomes - protein synthesis and transport.
  • Smooth ER (SER): No ribosomes - lipid synthesis and detoxification.

5.8 Golgi Apparatus

  • Packages and transports materials within the cell.
  • Modifies proteins and lipids.
  • Forms lysosomes and secretory vesicles.
  • Called the "post office" of the cell.

5.9 Lysosomes

  • Known as the "Suicide bags of the cell".
  • Contains digestive enzymes to break down waste.
  • Destroy old or damaged cell organelles.
  • Protect the cell from foreign invaders.

5.10 Vacuoles

  • Store water, waste, and other materials.
  • Plant cells have a large central vacuole.
  • Animal cells have small, temporary vacuoles.
  • Help maintain turgor pressure in plants.

5.11 Chloroplasts (Only in Plant Cells)

  • Site of photosynthesis.
  • Contains chlorophyll (green pigment).
  • Converts light energy into chemical energy (food).
  • Found only in green plants and some algae.
📝 Fun Fact:

Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA and are believed to have originated from primitive bacteria through endosymbiosis.

7. Functions of a Cell

  • Structural Support: Cells provide structure and shape to the body.
  • Transport: Movement of substances across the cell membrane.
  • Energy Production: Mitochondria produce energy (ATP).
  • Protein Synthesis: Ribosomes make proteins.
  • Reproduction: Cells divide to produce new cells (mitosis/meiosis).
  • Waste Removal: Lysosomes digest waste materials.
  • Response to Stimuli: Cells respond to changes in the environment.
  • Growth and Development: Cells grow and differentiate.

Chapter Summary

  • The cell is the basic unit of life, discovered by Robert Hooke in 1665.
  • Cell theory states that all living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and new cells arise from existing cells.
  • Cells are of two types: Prokaryotic (no nucleus) and Eukaryotic (with nucleus).
  • Eukaryotic cells contain organelles like nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, and vacuoles.
  • Mitochondria produce energy - called the "Powerhouse of the cell".
  • Nucleus controls all cell activities - called the "Brain of the cell".
  • Lysosomes digest waste - called the "Suicide bags of the cell".
  • Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts, and large vacuole; animal cells do not.
  • Chloroplasts are the site of photosynthesis (only in plants).
  • Cell membrane is selectively permeable - controls entry and exit of substances.
