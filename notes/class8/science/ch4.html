Chapter 4: Materials - Metals and Non-Metals

Everything around us is made of different materials. Some materials are hard, shiny, and can be beaten into thin sheets, while others are dull, brittle, and break easily. In this chapter, we will learn about the properties of metals and non-metals, their uses, and how they react with various substances.

Key Points

  • 🔩 Metals: Hard, shiny, malleable, ductile, good conductors of heat and electricity. Examples: Iron, Copper, Aluminium, Gold, Silver.
  • 💎 Non-Metals: Soft, dull, brittle, non-malleable, poor conductors of heat and electricity. Examples: Carbon, Sulphur, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Phosphorus.
  • 🔨 Malleability: Property of metals to be beaten into thin sheets.
  • 📏 Ductility: Property of metals to be drawn into thin wires.
  • 🔊 Sonorous: Metals produce a ringing sound when struck.
  • 💧 Reaction with Water: Some metals react with water to produce metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.
  • 🌬️ Reaction with Oxygen: Metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides (basic oxides). Non-metals react with oxygen to form non-metal oxides (acidic oxides).
  • 🧪 Reaction with Acids: Metals react with acids to produce salt and hydrogen gas. Non-metals generally do not react with acids.
  • 🔄 Displacement Reaction: A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.
  • 📊 Reactivity Series: Arrangement of metals in order of decreasing reactivity (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Pb, H, Cu, Ag, Au).
  • 🛡️ Noble Metals: Gold (Au), Silver (Ag), Platinum (Pt) - do not react easily with other substances.
  • 🔧 Uses of Metals: Making utensils, wires, machinery, jewellery, construction.
  • 🔬 Uses of Non-Metals: Oxygen (breathing), Nitrogen (fertilizers), Sulphur (medicines), Carbon (fuel).

1. Introduction

We use many different materials in our daily life - iron nails, copper wires, aluminium utensils, gold jewellery, plastic bottles, wooden chairs, etc. Based on their properties, materials can be broadly classified into two categories: Metals and Non-Metals.

Metals and non-metals have very different physical and chemical properties. Let us study them in detail.

📝 Examples of Metals:

Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Aluminium (Al), Gold (Au), Silver (Ag), Zinc (Zn), Lead (Pb), Tin (Sn), Sodium (Na), Potassium (K), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg)

📝 Examples of Non-Metals:

Carbon (C), Sulphur (S), Oxygen (O₂), Nitrogen (N₂), Hydrogen (H₂), Phosphorus (P), Chlorine (Cl₂), Iodine (I₂), Bromine (Br₂)

2.1 Detailed Explanation of Key Properties

A. Lustre (Shininess)

  • Metals: Have a shiny appearance. Example: Gold, Silver, Copper.
  • Non-Metals: Generally dull. Exception: Iodine (shiny) and Diamond (brilliant).

B. Malleability

  • Metals: Can be hammered into thin sheets. Example: Aluminium foil.
  • Non-Metals: Cannot be hammered; they break (brittle). Example: Coal, Sulphur.

C. Ductility

  • Metals: Can be drawn into thin wires. Example: Copper wire.
  • Non-Metals: Cannot be drawn into wires.

D. Conductivity

  • Metals: Good conductors of heat and electricity. Example: Copper, Aluminium used in wires.
  • Non-Metals: Poor conductors. Exception: Graphite (a form of carbon) conducts electricity.

E. Sonority

  • Metals: Produce a ringing sound when struck. Example: Bells are made of metal.
  • Non-Metals: Do not produce sound.
💡 Exceptions in Metals:
  • Sodium (Na) and Potassium (K) are soft metals that can be cut with a knife.
  • Mercury (Hg) is a liquid metal at room temperature.
  • Gallium (Ga) and Caesium (Cs) melt in your hand.
💡 Exceptions in Non-Metals:
  • Diamond is the hardest natural substance (non-metal).
  • Graphite is a good conductor of electricity (non-metal).
  • Iodine is a shiny non-metal.

3. Chemical Properties of Metals and Non-Metals

3.1 Reaction with Oxygen (Air)

Metals: Metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides which are basic in nature.

📝 Examples:
  • Copper + Oxygen → Copper(II) Oxide (black)
    2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO
  • Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium Oxide (white)
    2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
  • Aluminium + Oxygen → Aluminium Oxide
    4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃
  • Iron + Oxygen → Iron(III) Oxide (Rust)
    4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃

Non-Metals: Non-metals react with oxygen to form non-metal oxides which are acidic in nature.

📝 Examples:
  • Carbon + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)
    C + O₂ → CO₂
  • Sulphur + Oxygen → Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂)
    S + O₂ → SO₂
  • Phosphorus + Oxygen → Phosphorus Pentoxide (P₂O₅)
    4P + 5O₂ → 2P₂O₅

3.2 Reaction with Water

Metals: Different metals react with water at different rates.

MetalReaction with WaterProduct
Sodium (Na)Violent reaction, catches fireSodium hydroxide + Hydrogen
Potassium (K)Very violent, catches firePotassium hydroxide + Hydrogen
Calcium (Ca)Reacts vigorously, bubblesCalcium hydroxide + Hydrogen
Magnesium (Mg)Reacts slowly with hot waterMagnesium hydroxide + Hydrogen
Aluminium (Al)Reacts very slowlyAluminium hydroxide + Hydrogen
Zinc (Zn)Reacts with steamZinc oxide + Hydrogen
Iron (Fe)Reacts with steamIron oxide + Hydrogen
Copper (Cu)No reaction-
Gold (Au)No reaction-
📝 Chemical Equations:
  • 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
  • 2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂↑
  • Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑
  • Mg + H₂O (hot) → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂↑
  • 3Fe + 4H₂O (steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑

Non-Metals: Generally do not react with water (except some like chlorine which dissolve in water).

3.3 Reaction with Acids

Metals: Metals react with dilute acids to produce salt and hydrogen gas.

📝 Examples:
  • Zinc + Sulphuric Acid → Zinc Sulphate + Hydrogen
    Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
  • Iron + Hydrochloric Acid → Iron(II) Chloride + Hydrogen
    Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂↑
  • Magnesium + Hydrochloric Acid → Magnesium Chloride + Hydrogen
    Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑
  • Aluminium + Sulphuric Acid → Aluminium Sulphate + Hydrogen
    2Al + 3H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3H₂↑
💡 Important:

Copper, Silver, and Gold do not react with dilute acids. They are called noble metals.

Non-Metals: Generally do not react with dilute acids.

3.4 Reaction with Bases

Metals: Some metals react with bases to produce hydrogen gas.

📝 Example:

Aluminium + Sodium Hydroxide → Sodium Aluminate + Hydrogen
2Al + 2NaOH + 2H₂O → 2NaAlO₂ + 3H₂↑

Zinc + Sodium Hydroxide → Sodium Zincate + Hydrogen
Zn + 2NaOH → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂↑

Non-Metals: Non-metal oxides (acidic oxides) react with bases to form salt and water.

📝 Example:

Carbon Dioxide + Sodium Hydroxide → Sodium Carbonate + Water
CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O

3.5 Reaction with Salt Solutions (Displacement Reaction)

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.

📝 Examples:
  • Iron + Copper Sulphate (blue) → Iron Sulphate (green) + Copper
    Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
  • Zinc + Copper Sulphate → Zinc Sulphate + Copper
    Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

5. Displacement Reactions

A reaction in which a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution is called a displacement reaction.

Examples:

📝 Experiment 1: Iron + Copper Sulphate
  • Take Copper Sulphate solution (blue colour).
  • Add iron nails to it.
  • After some time, the blue colour fades and the solution turns green.
  • A reddish-brown deposit of copper forms on the iron nail.
  • Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate.

Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu

📝 Experiment 2: Zinc + Copper Sulphate

Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

💡 Important:

If we add copper to Iron Sulphate solution, no reaction occurs because copper is less reactive than iron and cannot displace it.

Chapter Summary

  • Materials are classified into Metals and Non-Metals based on their properties.
  • Metals are lustrous, malleable, ductile, sonorous, and good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Non-metals are dull, brittle, non-malleable, non-ductile, non-sonorous, and poor conductors (except graphite).
  • Metals react with oxygen to form basic oxides.
  • Non-metals react with oxygen to form acidic oxides.
  • Some metals react with water to produce metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.
  • Metals react with acids to produce salt and hydrogen gas.
  • Displacement reaction: More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its salt solution.
  • Reactivity series: List of metals arranged in order of decreasing reactivity (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Pb, H, Cu, Ag, Au, Pt).
  • Noble metals (Au, Ag, Pt) do not react easily with other substances.
  • Metals are used in construction, wires, utensils, machinery, and jewellery.
  • Non-metals are used in fertilizers, medicines, fuels, and water purification.
