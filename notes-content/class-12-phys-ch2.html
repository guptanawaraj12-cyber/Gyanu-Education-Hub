1. Introduction to Periodic Motion

Periodic Motion is a type of motion that repeats itself after a fixed interval of time. The interval of time after which the motion repeats is called the time period (T).

Examples of Periodic Motion:

  • Motion of a pendulum
  • Motion of a mass attached to a spring
  • Rotation of the Earth
  • Motion of the hands of a clock
  • Vibration of a tuning fork

1.1 Key Terms

  • Oscillation/Vibration: One complete to-and-fro motion.
  • Time Period (T): Time taken for one complete oscillation.
  • Frequency (f): Number of oscillations per second. f = 1/T
  • Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement from mean position.
  • Phase (φ): The state of motion at a given instant.
📌 Key Point: Periodic motion is the foundation of oscillatory motion. All oscillatory motions are periodic, but not all periodic motions are oscillatory (e.g., circular motion).

2. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is the simplest form of periodic motion. It is the motion in which the acceleration of the particle is directly proportional to its displacement from the mean position and is always directed towards the mean position.

2.1 Definition of SHM

A particle is said to execute SHM if its acceleration is:
  • Directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position
  • Always directed towards the mean position
a ∝ −x    or    a = −ω²x

Where ω is the angular frequency (or angular velocity) of the motion.

2.2 Characteristics of SHM

  • Mean Position: The position where the particle has zero acceleration and zero restoring force.
  • Extreme Position: The position where the particle has maximum acceleration and maximum restoring force.
  • Restoring Force: The force that tries to bring the particle back to the mean position.
  • Restoring Force (F): F = −kx, where k is the force constant.
📌 Key Point: The negative sign in a = −ω²x indicates that the acceleration is always directed towards the mean position (opposite to displacement).

3. Derivation of SHM Equation

Let us derive the equation of SHM from the definition of restoring force.

1 From Hooke's Law, the restoring force is: F = −kx
2 From Newton's Second Law: F = ma
3 Equating both: ma = −kx
4 Since a = d²x/dt²:
m (d²x/dt²) = −kx
5 Rearranging:
d²x/dt² + (k/m)x = 0
6 Let ω² = k/m
7 Therefore:
d²x/dt² + ω²x = 0
💡 This is the fundamental differential equation of SHM.

4. Differential Equation of SHM

The differential equation of SHM is:

d²x/dt² + ω²x = 0

Where ω is the angular frequency, given by:

ω = √(k/m)

The general solution to this differential equation is:

x = A sin(ωt + φ)

or

x = A cos(ωt + φ)

Where:

  • A = Amplitude (maximum displacement)
  • ω = Angular frequency
  • φ = Phase constant
  • t = Time

5. Solution of Differential Equation

To solve the differential equation d²x/dt² + ω²x = 0, we use the method of trial solution.

1 Assume a solution of the form: x = A sin(ωt + φ)
2 Differentiate with respect to t:
dx/dt = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
3 Differentiate again:
d²x/dt² = −Aω² sin(ωt + φ)
4 Substitute back into the differential equation:
−Aω² sin(ωt + φ) + ω²[A sin(ωt + φ)] = 0
5 Simplify:
−Aω² sin(ωt + φ) + Aω² sin(ωt + φ) = 0
6 Which is true for all t.
7 Therefore, the solution is:
x = A sin(ωt + φ)

If the particle starts from the mean position (x = 0 at t = 0), then φ = 0:

x = A sin(ωt)

If the particle starts from the extreme position (x = A at t = 0), then φ = π/2:

x = A cos(ωt)

6. Velocity in SHM

The velocity of a particle executing SHM can be derived from the displacement equation.

6.1 Velocity from Displacement

Given x = A sin(ωt + φ), the velocity is:

1 x = A sin(ωt + φ)
2 Differentiate with respect to t:
v = dx/dt = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
3 Therefore:
v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)

6.2 Velocity in Terms of Displacement

1 We know that: v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
2 Also: x = A sin(ωt + φ)
3 Square both:
v² = A²ω² cos²(ωt + φ)
x² = A² sin²(ωt + φ)
4 Using cos²θ + sin²θ = 1:
v²/A²ω² + x²/A² = 1
5 Therefore:
v² = ω²(A² − x²)
v = ω√(A² − x²)

6.3 Maximum and Minimum Velocity

  • Maximum velocity (vmax): At mean position (x = 0)
    • vmax = Aω
  • Minimum velocity (vmin): At extreme position (x = A)
    • vmin = 0

7. Acceleration in SHM

The acceleration of a particle executing SHM can be derived from the velocity or displacement.

7.1 Acceleration from Displacement

1 x = A sin(ωt + φ)
2 Velocity: v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
3 Acceleration: a = dv/dt = −Aω² sin(ωt + φ)
4 Therefore:
a = −ω²x

7.2 Maximum and Minimum Acceleration

  • Maximum acceleration (amax): At extreme position (x = A)
    • amax = Aω²
  • Minimum acceleration (amin): At mean position (x = 0)
    • amin = 0
📌 Key Point: In SHM, acceleration is maximum at the extreme positions and zero at the mean position. Velocity is maximum at the mean position and zero at the extreme positions.

8. Time Period and Frequency

The time period of SHM is the time taken to complete one complete oscillation.

8.1 Time Period (T)

1 In one complete oscillation, the angular displacement is 2π radians.
2 Angular velocity (ω) = 2π/T
3 Therefore:
T = 2π/ω

8.2 Frequency (f)

Frequency is the number of oscillations per second.

f = 1/T = ω/2π
💡 Key Point: The time period of SHM does NOT depend on the amplitude. This is called isochronism.

9. Energy in SHM

A particle executing SHM has both kinetic energy and potential energy. The total energy remains constant throughout the motion.

9.1 Kinetic Energy (KE)

KE = ½mv² = ½mω²(A² − x²)

9.2 Potential Energy (PE)

PE = ½kx² = ½mω²x²

9.3 Total Energy (TE)

1 TE = KE + PE
2 TE = ½mω²(A² − x²) + ½mω²x²
3 TE = ½mω²A²
TE = ½mω²A² = ½kA²

9.4 Energy Distribution

  • At mean position (x = 0): KE = maximum, PE = 0
  • At extreme position (x = A): KE = 0, PE = maximum

10. Spring-Mass System

A spring-mass system is one of the simplest examples of SHM. When a mass is attached to a spring and displaced from its equilibrium position, it oscillates with SHM.

10.1 Restoring Force

According to Hooke's Law, the restoring force is:

F = −kx

Where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium.

11. Derivation of Time Period for Spring

1 The restoring force is: F = −kx
2 From Newton's Second Law: F = ma
3 Therefore: ma = −kx
4 Since a = d²x/dt²:
m(d²x/dt²) = −kx
5 Rearranging:
d²x/dt² + (k/m)x = 0
6 Comparing with the standard SHM equation: d²x/dt² + ω²x = 0
7 Therefore: ω² = k/m
8 ω = √(k/m)
9 Since T = 2π/ω:
T = 2π√(m/k)
💡 Important: The time period of a spring-mass system depends on:
  • Mass (m): T ∝ √m
  • Spring constant (k): T ∝ 1/√k

12. Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum consists of a point mass (called the bob) attached to a light, inextensible string of length L.

12.1 Restoring Force

The restoring force is the component of the weight along the tangent to the circular path.

F = −mg sinθ

For small angles (θ < 15°), sinθ ≈ θ:

F = −mgθ

12.2 Relation between θ and x

For small angles, θ ≈ x/L:

F = −mg(x/L)

13. Derivation of Time Period for Pendulum

1 The restoring force is: F = −mg sinθ
2 For small θ, sinθ ≈ θ:
F = −mgθ
3 Since θ = x/L:
F = −mg(x/L)
4 This is of the form F = −kx, where k = mg/L
5 The time period of a spring-mass system is: T = 2π√(m/k)
6 Substituting k = mg/L:
T = 2π√(m/(mg/L))
T = 2π√(L/g)
T = 2π√(L/g)
💡 Important: The time period of a simple pendulum depends on:
  • Length (L): T ∝ √L
  • Acceleration due to gravity (g): T ∝ 1/√g
  • Does NOT depend on: Mass of the bob
  • Does NOT depend on: Amplitude (for small angles)

14. Horizontal Oscillation of a Spring

In horizontal oscillation, the mass moves on a frictionless horizontal surface. The spring is attached to a fixed wall at one end and a mass at the other.

14.1 Equation of Motion

1 The restoring force: F = −kx
2 From Newton's Second Law: ma = −kx
3 Therefore: d²x/dt² + (k/m)x = 0
4 The solution is: x = A sin(ωt + φ)
5 Where: ω = √(k/m)
6 Time period: T = 2π√(m/k)

15. Vertical Oscillation of a Spring

In vertical oscillation, the mass is attached to a spring hanging vertically. The spring stretches due to the weight of the mass.

15.1 Equilibrium Position

At equilibrium, the spring force equals the weight:

kx₀ = mg

Where x₀ is the extension at equilibrium.

15.2 Time Period

1 At equilibrium: kx₀ = mg
2 When displaced by x from equilibrium:
F = −kx
3 The equation of motion is the same as horizontal:
d²x/dt² + (k/m)x = 0
4 Therefore:
T = 2π√(m/k)
📌 Key Point: The time period of vertical oscillation is the SAME as horizontal oscillation for the same spring and mass.

16. Compound Pendulum

A compound pendulum (or physical pendulum) is a rigid body that oscillates about a fixed axis passing through a point other than its center of mass.

16.1 Time Period of Compound Pendulum

1 The equation of motion is: τ = Iα
2 The restoring torque is: τ = −MgL sinθ
3 For small θ, sinθ ≈ θ:
τ = −MgLθ
4 Therefore: Iα = −MgLθ
5 Since α = d²θ/dt²:
I(d²θ/dt²) + MgLθ = 0
6 d²θ/dt² + (MgL/I)θ = 0
7 Comparing with SHM: ω² = MgL/I
8 Therefore:
T = 2π√(I/(MgL))
📌 Key Point: A simple pendulum is a special case of a compound pendulum where all the mass is concentrated at a point (I = ML²).

17. Energy Conservation in SHM

In SHM, total energy is always conserved. Let's prove this.

1 We know: x = A sin(ωt + φ)
2 Velocity: v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
3 Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv² = ½m(Aω cos(ωt + φ))²
KE = ½mω²A² cos²(ωt + φ)
4 Potential Energy: PE = ½kx² = ½k(A sin(ωt + φ))²
PE = ½kA² sin²(ωt + φ)
5 Since k = mω²:
PE = ½mω²A² sin²(ωt + φ)
6 Total Energy: TE = KE + PE
TE = ½mω²A²[cos²(ωt + φ) + sin²(ωt + φ)]
7 Using cos²θ + sin²θ = 1:
TE = ½mω²A²
8 Therefore:
TE = ½mω²A² = constant
💡 Key Point: In SHM, energy is continuously converted between kinetic and potential forms, but the total energy remains constant.

18. Important Formulas

a = −ω²x (Acceleration in SHM)
x = A sin(ωt + φ) (Displacement)
v = Aω cos(ωt + φ) (Velocity)
v = ω√(A² − x²) (Velocity in terms of displacement)
vmax = Aω (Maximum velocity)
amax = Aω² (Maximum acceleration)
T = 2π/ω (Time Period)
f = 1/T = ω/2π (Frequency)
KE = ½mω²(A² − x²) (Kinetic Energy)
PE = ½mω²x² (Potential Energy)
TE = ½mω²A² = ½kA² (Total Energy)
T = 2π√(m/k) (Spring-Mass System)
T = 2π√(L/g) (Simple Pendulum)
T = 2π√(I/(MgL)) (Compound Pendulum)

19. Solved Examples

Example 1:
A particle executes SHM with amplitude 5 cm and time period 2 s. Find the maximum velocity and maximum acceleration.

Solution:
Given: A = 5 cm = 0.05 m, T = 2 s
ω = 2π/T = 2π/2 = π rad/s
vmax = Aω = 0.05 × π = 0.157 m/s
amax = Aω² = 0.05 × π² = 0.493 m/s²
Example 2:
A spring-mass system has a mass of 0.5 kg and spring constant 20 N/m. Find the time period.

Solution:
Given: m = 0.5 kg, k = 20 N/m
T = 2π√(m/k) = 2π√(0.5/20) = 2π√0.025 = 2π × 0.158 = 0.993 s
Example 3:
A simple pendulum has a length of 1 m. Calculate its time period on Earth (g = 9.8 m/s²).

Solution:
Given: L = 1 m, g = 9.8 m/s²
T = 2π√(L/g) = 2π√(1/9.8) = 2π√0.102 = 2π × 0.319 = 2.006 s
Example 4:
A particle executing SHM has displacement x = 0.02 sin(4πt). Find:
(a) Amplitude
(b) Angular frequency
(c) Time period

Solution:
Comparing with x = A sin(ωt):
(a) A = 0.02 m = 2 cm
(b) ω = 4π rad/s
(c) T = 2π/ω = 2π/4π = 0.5 s

21. Applications of Periodic Motion

🔧 1. Pendulum Clocks: The simple pendulum is used in pendulum clocks to measure time. The time period is constant, making it a reliable timekeeper.
🔧 2. Springs in Vehicles: Springs are used in vehicle suspension systems to absorb shocks. They oscillate and dampen the motion of the vehicle.
🔧 3. Seismometers: Used to measure earthquakes. The mass-spring system oscillates during ground motion, and the displacement is recorded.
🔧 4. Tuning Forks: Used to produce standard frequencies in music and physics experiments. The prongs oscillate in SHM.
🔧 5. Bridges and Buildings: Engineers consider periodic motion when designing buildings and bridges to avoid resonance that could cause collapse.
🔧 6. Atomic and Molecular Vibrations: Atoms in molecules vibrate in SHM, which is studied in spectroscopy.
🔧 7. Heartbeat: The rhythmic contraction and relaxation of the heart is a form of periodic motion.
🔧 8. Sound Waves: Sound is produced by periodic vibrations of particles in a medium.
📌 Summary: Periodic motion is everywhere in nature and technology. Understanding SHM is essential for fields ranging from engineering to medicine.
