1. Introduction to Rotational Dynamics

Rotational dynamics is the branch of classical mechanics that deals with the motion of rigid bodies rotating about a fixed axis. It is the rotational analogue of translational dynamics (linear motion).

A rigid body is defined as a body in which the distance between any two particles remains constant during motion. In rotational motion, every particle of the body moves in a circular path about a fixed axis, with the same angular displacement, angular velocity, and angular acceleration.

1.1 Analogies Between Linear and Rotational Motion

The following table shows the analogy between linear and rotational quantities:

📌 Key Point: The beauty of rotational dynamics is that all linear equations have exact rotational counterparts. Understanding this analogy makes learning rotational dynamics much easier.

2. Angular Kinematics

2.1 Angular Displacement (θ)

The angle through which a body rotates about a fixed axis is called angular displacement. It is measured in radians (rad).

θ = s / r

Where s is the arc length and r is the radius of the circular path.

Note: 1 revolution = 2π radians = 360°

2.2 Angular Velocity (ω)

The rate of change of angular displacement with respect to time is called angular velocity.

ω = dθ / dt

For uniform circular motion, angular velocity is constant:

ω = 2π / T = 2πf

Where T is the time period and f is the frequency.

2.3 Angular Acceleration (α)

The rate of change of angular velocity with respect to time is called angular acceleration.

α = dω / dt

When α is constant, the motion is said to have uniform angular acceleration.

3. Relation Between Linear and Angular Quantities

For a particle moving in a circular path of radius r, the relations between linear and angular quantities are:

3.1 Linear Displacement

s = rθ

3.2 Linear Velocity

v = rω

3.3 Tangential Acceleration

at = rα

3.4 Centripetal Acceleration

ac = rω² = v²/r
💡 Key Point: In circular motion, there are two types of acceleration:
  • Tangential acceleration (at): Changes the speed (magnitude of velocity)
  • Centripetal acceleration (ac): Changes the direction of velocity

4. Derivation of Angular Motion Equations

For uniform angular acceleration (α = constant), the equations of angular motion are derived below.

4.1 First Equation: ω = ω₀ + αt

1 Angular acceleration is defined as: α = dω / dt
2 Rearranging: dω = α dt
3 Integrating both sides from initial to final:
∫ω₀ω dω = ∫0t α dt
4 Since α is constant:
[ω]ω₀ω = α [t]0t
5 Evaluating:
ω − ω₀ = αt
6 Therefore:
ω = ω₀ + αt

4.2 Second Equation: θ = ω₀t + ½αt²

1 Angular velocity is defined as: ω = dθ / dt
2 Substituting ω = ω₀ + αt:
dθ / dt = ω₀ + αt
3 Rearranging: dθ = (ω₀ + αt) dt
4 Integrating from initial to final:
∫0θ dθ = ∫0t (ω₀ + αt) dt
5 Evaluating:
[θ]0θ = [ω₀t + ½αt²]0t
θ = ω₀t + ½αt²
6 Therefore:
θ = ω₀t + ½αt²

4.3 Third Equation: ω² = ω₀² + 2αθ

1 From the definition: α = dω / dt and ω = dθ / dt
2 We can write: α = ω dω / dθ
3 Rearranging: ω dω = α dθ
4 Integrating:
∫ω₀ω ω dω = ∫0θ α dθ
5 Evaluating:
[ω²/2]ω₀ω = [αθ]0θ
(ω²/2) − (ω₀²/2) = αθ
6 Multiplying both sides by 2:
ω² − ω₀² = 2αθ
7 Therefore:
ω² = ω₀² + 2αθ

5. Moment of Inertia

Moment of inertia (I) is the rotational analogue of mass in linear motion. It is a measure of an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion.

5.1 Definition

The moment of inertia of a body about a given axis is defined as the sum of the products of the mass of each particle and the square of its perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation.

I = Σ miri²

For a continuous body:

I = ∫ r² dm

5.2 Factors Affecting Moment of Inertia

  • Mass of the body: I ∝ M (more mass → more inertia)
  • Distribution of mass: I ∝ r² (mass farther from axis → more inertia)
  • Shape of the body: Different shapes have different I for the same mass
  • Axis of rotation: I is different for different axes

5.3 Physical Significance of Moment of Inertia

Moment of inertia is a measure of the rotational inertia of a body. The larger the moment of inertia, the more difficult it is to:

  • Start the body rotating (if at rest)
  • Stop the body rotating (if already rotating)
  • Change the angular velocity of the body
💡 Key Point: Moment of inertia depends on the distribution of mass about the axis, not just the total mass. A body with mass concentrated at the center has a smaller moment of inertia than a body with mass spread out.

6. Derivation of Moment of Inertia Formulas

6.1 Moment of Inertia of a Uniform Rod

6.1.1 About an Axis Through the Center (Perpendicular to the Rod)

Consider a uniform rod of length L and mass M. The axis passes through the center of mass (O) and is perpendicular to the rod.

1 Linear mass density: λ = M/L
2 Consider a small element of length dx at distance x from the center:
dm = λ dx
3 Moment of inertia of this element:
dI = x² dm = x²λ dx
4 Total moment of inertia:
I = ∫ dI = ∫−L/2L/2 x²λ dx
5 Substituting λ = M/L:
I = (M/L) ∫−L/2L/2 x² dx
6 Evaluating the integral:
I = (M/L) [x³/3]−L/2L/2
I = (M/L) [(L/2)³/3 − (−L/2)³/3]
I = (M/L) [(L³/8)/3 − (−L³/8)/3]
I = (M/L) [L³/24 − (−L³/24)]
I = (M/L) [L³/12]
7 Therefore:
Icenter = ¹⁄₁₂ ML²

6.1.2 About an Axis Through One End (Perpendicular to the Rod)

Consider a uniform rod of length L and mass M. The axis passes through one end (A) and is perpendicular to the rod.

1 Linear mass density: λ = M/L
2 Consider a small element of length dx at distance x from the end A:
dm = λ dx
3 Moment of inertia of this element:
dI = x² dm = x²λ dx
4 Total moment of inertia:
I = ∫ dI = ∫0L x²λ dx
5 Substituting λ = M/L:
I = (M/L) ∫0L x² dx
6 Evaluating the integral:
I = (M/L) [x³/3]0L
I = (M/L) [L³/3]
7 Therefore:
Iend = ⅓ ML²

6.2 Moment of Inertia of a Solid Sphere

Consider a solid sphere of radius R and mass M. The axis passes through the diameter.

1 Volume of sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃πR³
2 Mass density: ρ = M/V = 3M/(4πR³)
3 Consider a spherical shell of radius r and thickness dr:
dV = 4πr² dr
dm = ρ dV = 4πρr² dr
4 Moment of inertia of a spherical shell about diameter: dI = ⅔r² dm
5 Total moment of inertia:
I = ∫ dI = ∫0R ⅔r²(4πρr² dr)
I = ⅔(4πρ) ∫0R r⁴ dr
I = ⁸⁄₃πρ [r⁵/5]0R
I = ⁸⁄₃πρ (R⁵/5)
6 Substituting ρ = 3M/(4πR³):
I = ⁸⁄₃π × 3M/(4πR³) × (R⁵/5)
I = ⅖ MR²
7 Therefore:
Isolid sphere = ⅖ MR²

6.3 Moment of Inertia of a Solid Disc

Consider a solid disc of radius R and mass M. The axis passes through the center and is perpendicular to the plane of the disc.

1 Area of disc: A = πR²
2 Area density: σ = M/πR²
3 Consider a ring of radius r and thickness dr:
dA = 2πr dr
dm = σ dA = 2πσr dr
4 Moment of inertia of the ring about the center: dI = r² dm
dI = r²(2πσr dr) = 2πσr³ dr
5 Total moment of inertia:
I = ∫ dI = ∫0R 2πσr³ dr
I = 2πσ [r⁴/4]0R
I = 2πσ (R⁴/4) = ½πσR⁴
6 Substituting σ = M/πR²:
I = ½π(M/πR²)R⁴ = ½MR²
7 Therefore:
Idisc = ½ MR²

6.4 Moment of Inertia of a Thin Hoop/Ring

Consider a thin hoop of radius R and mass M. The axis passes through the center and is perpendicular to the plane of the hoop.

1 All mass is at distance R from the axis.
2 Moment of inertia: I = Σ miR² = R²Σmi = MR²
3 Therefore:
Ihoop = MR²

7. Radius of Gyration

The radius of gyration (K) of a body about an axis of rotation is defined as the distance from the axis at which the entire mass of the body can be assumed to be concentrated to give the same moment of inertia.

I = MK²
K = √(I / M)

Where M is the total mass of the body and I is its moment of inertia.

7.1 Examples of Radius of Gyration

Body Moment of Inertia Radius of Gyration
Hoop (axis through center)MR²K = R
Disc (axis through center)½MR²K = R/√2
Solid Sphere⅖MR²K = R√(2/5)
Rod (center axis)¹⁄₁₂ML²K = L/(2√3)
📌 Key Point: The radius of gyration depends on the distribution of mass about the axis of rotation. For a given body, it is different for different axes. It is measured in meters (m).

8. Torque and Angular Acceleration

Torque (τ) is the rotational analogue of force. It is the turning effect of a force.

8.1 Definition of Torque

Torque is defined as the product of the force and the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation (moment arm).

τ = F × r = Fr sinθ

Where:

  • F = magnitude of the force
  • r = distance from the axis to the point of application
  • θ = angle between the force vector and the position vector

8.2 Vector Form of Torque

τ = r × F

The direction of torque is given by the right-hand rule.

8.3 Relation Between Torque and Angular Acceleration

For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis, the torque is related to angular acceleration by:

τ = Iα

This is the rotational analogue of Newton's second law (F = ma).

💡 Key Point: Just as force causes linear acceleration, torque causes angular acceleration. The moment of inertia plays the same role in rotation that mass plays in linear motion.

9. Full Derivation of τ = Iα

1 Consider a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis with angular acceleration α.
2 For a particle of mass mi at distance ri from the axis:
3 The tangential acceleration of the particle: ati = riα
4 The tangential force on the particle: Fi = miati = miriα
5 The torque on the particle: τi = Firi
τi = (miriα) × ri = miri²α
6 The total torque on the body is the sum of torques on all particles:
τ = Στi = Σ(miri²α)
7 Since α is constant for all particles:
τ = α Σmiri²
8 But Σmiri² = I (moment of inertia)
9 Therefore:
τ = Iα

10. Work and Power in Rotational Motion

10.1 Work Done by a Torque

The work done by a constant torque in rotating a body through an angular displacement θ is:

W = τθ

For variable torque:

W = ∫ τ dθ

10.2 Rotational Kinetic Energy

The kinetic energy of a rotating body is:

KErot = ½Iω²

10.3 Work-Energy Theorem for Rotation

The work done by a torque is equal to the change in rotational kinetic energy:

W = ½Iω² − ½Iω₀² = ΔKErot

10.4 Power in Rotational Motion

Power is the rate of doing work. In rotational motion:

P = dW/dt = τω
📌 Key Point: Work and power in rotational motion are directly analogous to translational motion: W = τθ and P = τω.

11. Angular Momentum

11.1 Definition

Angular momentum (L) is the rotational analogue of linear momentum. For a rotating rigid body:

L = Iω

Where I is the moment of inertia and ω is the angular velocity.

11.2 Vector Form

L = r × p = r × (mv)

For a particle moving in a circle:

L = mvr = mr²ω = Iω

11.3 Relation Between Torque and Angular Momentum

The torque acting on a body is equal to the rate of change of its angular momentum:

τ = dL/dt

This is the rotational analogue of Newton's second law in terms of momentum (F = dp/dt).

12. Conservation of Angular Momentum

12.1 Statement

The law of conservation of angular momentum states that:

If no external torque acts on a system, the total angular momentum of the system remains constant.

12.2 Derivation

1 We know that: τ = dL/dt
2 If no external torque acts: τ = 0
dL/dt = 0
3 Integrating both sides:
L = constant
4 Therefore:
I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂

12.3 Examples of Conservation of Angular Momentum

  • Ice Skater: Spins faster when arms are pulled in (I decreases → ω increases)
  • Diver: Tucks body to spin faster during a dive
  • Earth: Rotates with constant angular momentum (no external torque)
  • Gyroscope: Maintains its orientation due to conservation of angular momentum
  • Spinning Top: Continues to spin upright
💡 Real Life Application: Ice skaters pull their arms in to spin faster! When they extend their arms, they spin slower. This is conservation of angular momentum in action.

13. Kinetic Energy of a Rolling Body

A rolling body has both translational and rotational kinetic energy.

KEtotal = KEtranslational + KErotational
KEtotal = ½Mv² + ½Iω²

For a rolling body without slipping, the relation between linear velocity (v) and angular velocity (ω) is:

v = rω

13.1 Kinetic Energy for Different Rolling Bodies

Solid Sphere (I = ⅖MR²)

KE = ½Mv² + ½(⅖MR²)(v/R)²
KE = ½Mv² + ⅕Mv²
KE = ⁷⁄₁₀ Mv²

Disc (I = ½MR²)

KE = ½Mv² + ½(½MR²)(v/R)²
KE = ½Mv² + ¼Mv²
KE = ¾ Mv²

Hoop/Ring (I = MR²)

KE = ½Mv² + ½(MR²)(v/R)²
KE = ½Mv² + ½Mv²
KE = Mv²
📌 Key Point: For the same mass and radius, a solid sphere has the smallest total kinetic energy for a given speed, which is why it rolls down an incline faster than a disc or hoop.

14. Theorems of Moment of Inertia

14.1 Parallel Axis Theorem

The moment of inertia of a body about any axis is equal to the moment of inertia about a parallel axis through its center of mass plus the product of the mass and the square of the distance between the axes.

I = Icm + Md²

Where:

  • I = Moment of inertia about the given axis
  • Icm = Moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass (parallel to the given axis)
  • M = Total mass of the body
  • d = Distance between the two axes

14.2 Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For a planar body (lamina), the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the plane is equal to the sum of the moments of inertia about two mutually perpendicular axes in the plane that intersect at the point where the perpendicular axis passes through.

Iz = Ix + Iy

15. Derivations of Theorems

15.1 Derivation of Parallel Axis Theorem

1 Consider a body of mass M. Let the center of mass be at O.
2 Consider an axis through O (center of mass) and another parallel axis through P, at distance d from O.
3 For a particle of mass mi at position ri from O:
Icm = Σmiri²
4 Distance of the particle from the axis through P is ri + d
I = Σmi(ri + d)²
I = Σmi(ri² + 2rid + d²)
I = Σmiri² + 2dΣmiri + d²Σmi
5 But Σmiri = 0 (since O is the center of mass)
I = Σmiri² + d²M
6 Therefore:
I = Icm + Md²

15.2 Derivation of Perpendicular Axis Theorem

1 Consider a planar body (lamina) lying in the xy-plane.
2 The z-axis is perpendicular to the plane through point O.
3 For a particle at position (x, y) with mass mi:
Iz = Σmi(x² + y²) = Σmix² + Σmiy²
4 But Ix = Σmiy² and Iy = Σmix²
5 Therefore:
Iz = Ix + Iy

17. Solved Examples

Example 1:
A wheel rotates with a constant angular acceleration of 4 rad/s². If the initial angular velocity is 2 rad/s, find:
(a) The angular displacement after 3 seconds.
(b) The final angular velocity after 3 seconds.

Solution:
Given: ω₀ = 2 rad/s, α = 4 rad/s², t = 3 s

(a) Using θ = ω₀t + ½αt²
θ = 2(3) + ½(4)(3)²
θ = 6 + ½(4)(9)
θ = 6 + 18 = 24 rad

(b) Using ω = ω₀ + αt
ω = 2 + 4(3)
ω = 2 + 12 = 14 rad/s
Example 2:
A solid sphere of mass 5 kg and radius 0.2 m has a moment of inertia I = ⅖MR². Find its radius of gyration.

Solution:
I = ⅖ × 5 × (0.2)² = ⅖ × 5 × 0.04 = 0.08 kg·m²

K = √(I / M) = √(0.08 / 5) = √0.016 = 0.126 m
Example 3:
A wheel of moment of inertia 2.5 kg·m² is subjected to a torque of 15 N·m. Find the angular acceleration.

Solution:
Using τ = Iα
15 = 2.5 × α
α = 15 / 2.5 = 6 rad/s²
Example 4:
A motor develops a torque of 20 N·m at an angular speed of 30 rad/s. Calculate the power developed.

Solution:
P = τω = 20 × 30 = 600 W
Example 5:
A figure skater spins with arms extended, with moment of inertia 6 kg·m² and angular velocity 2 rad/s. When she pulls her arms in, her moment of inertia becomes 3 kg·m². Find her new angular velocity.

Solution:
Using conservation of angular momentum: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂
6 × 2 = 3 × ω₂
ω₂ = 12/3 = 4 rad/s
Example 6:
A solid sphere of mass 2 kg and radius 0.1 m rolls with a speed of 5 m/s. Find its total kinetic energy.

Solution:
For a solid sphere, I = ⅖MR² = ⅖ × 2 × (0.1)² = 0.008 kg·m²
ω = v/R = 5/0.1 = 50 rad/s
KEtotal = ½Mv² + ½Iω²
= ½(2)(5)² + ½(0.008)(50)²
= 25 + 10 = 35 J
Example 7:
A uniform rod of length 1 m and mass 2 kg has a moment of inertia about its center of ¹⁄₁₂ML² = ¹⁄₆ kg·m². Find its moment of inertia about an axis through one end (parallel to the center axis).

Solution:
Using parallel axis theorem: I = Icm + Md²
I = ¹⁄₆ + 2 × (0.5)²
I = ¹⁄₆ + 2 × 0.25
I = ¹⁄₆ + 0.5 = ¹⁄₆ + ³⁄₆ = ⁴⁄₆ = ⅔ kg·m²

19. Real-Life Applications

🔧 1. Flywheels in Machines:
Flywheels store rotational energy and help maintain smooth operation in engines and machines. They resist changes in rotational speed due to their high moment of inertia. Used in automobiles, generators, and industrial machinery.
🔧 2. Gyroscopes:
Used in navigation systems, smartphones, drones, and spacecraft. They maintain orientation due to conservation of angular momentum. A spinning gyroscope resists changes in its axis of rotation.
🔧 3. Ice Skating:
Skaters use conservation of angular momentum to spin faster or slower by changing their moment of inertia. When they pull their arms in, they spin faster; when they extend their arms, they spin slower.
🔧 4. Diving and Gymnastics:
Athletes tuck their bodies (reducing I) to increase angular velocity during spins and flips. This allows them to perform multiple rotations before entering the water or landing.
🔧 5. Washing Machines:
The spinning drum uses rotational dynamics to extract water from clothes through centrifugal force. The high-speed rotation creates a large centripetal force that pushes water outward.
🔧 6. Automobile Wheels:
Understanding rotational dynamics helps design better tires, brakes, and suspension systems. The moment of inertia of wheels affects acceleration and braking performance.
🔧 7. Earth's Rotation:
The Earth rotates with constant angular momentum. Its rotation is a classic example of conservation of angular momentum. The Earth's rotation affects weather patterns, ocean currents, and the length of day and night.
🔧 8. Bicycles and Motorcycles:
The spinning wheels of a bicycle provide gyroscopic stability, helping the rider maintain balance. This is due to the conservation of angular momentum of the wheels.
🔧 9. Satellites and Spacecraft:
Satellites use reaction wheels (flywheels) to control their orientation in space without using fuel. By spinning a wheel in one direction, the satellite rotates in the opposite direction due to conservation of angular momentum.
🔧 10. Sports:
Many sports use rotational dynamics principles:
  • Cricket: Spin bowling
  • Tennis: Topspin and backspin shots
  • Baseball: Curveballs
  • Golf: Club swing mechanics
📌 Summary: Rotational dynamics is everywhere in everyday life – from sports to machines to space exploration. Understanding these concepts helps us design better technology and appreciate the physics of motion around us!
