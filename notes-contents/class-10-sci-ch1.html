Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

🔬 Science • Class 10 • 📅 Updated: July 10, 2026

1. Introduction

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are transformed into one or more new substances (products). During a chemical reaction, bonds between atoms break and new bonds form.

📌 Key Point: In a chemical reaction, atoms are neither created nor destroyed. They are simply rearranged. This is the Law of Conservation of Mass.

2. Observations of Chemical Reactions

You can identify a chemical reaction by observing one or more of these changes:

  • Change in state: Solid → Liquid → Gas (e.g., burning of candle wax).
  • Change in color: Green iron nails turn reddish-brown when kept in copper sulphate solution.
  • Evolution of gas: Bubbles or fizzing (e.g., zinc + hydrochloric acid → hydrogen gas).
  • Change in temperature: Exothermic (releases heat) or Endothermic (absorbs heat).
  • Formation of a precipitate: A solid forms from two liquids (e.g., AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl ↓).

3. Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulas and symbols.

3.1 Reactants and Products

  • Reactants: The substances that react together (left side of the arrow).
  • Products: The new substances formed (right side of the arrow).
Reactants → Products
Example:
Hydrogen + Oxygen → Water
2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

3.2 Balanced vs Unbalanced Equations

A balanced equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides. This follows the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Unbalanced: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O (Oxygen atoms: 2 on left, 1 on right)

Balanced: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (4 H atoms, 2 O atoms on both sides)

4. How to Balance a Chemical Equation

Step-by-step method (Hit and Trial):

  1. Write the unbalanced equation (skeleton equation).
  2. Count the number of atoms of each element on both sides.
  3. Add coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) to balance atoms.
  4. Never change subscripts inside a chemical formula.
  5. Start with the most complex molecule first, then balance the rest.
Example:
Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 1: Count atoms:
Left: Fe=1, H=2, O=1  |  Right: Fe=3, H=2, O=4

Step 2: Balance Fe: 3Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
Step 3: Balance O: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
Step 4: Balance H: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

Final Balanced Equation:
3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

5. Types of Chemical Reactions

5.1 Combination Reaction

Two or more substances combine to form a single product.

A + B → AB
Example:
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (Magnesium burns in oxygen to form magnesium oxide)

5.2 Decomposition Reaction

A single substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

AB → A + B
Example:
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (Heating calcium carbonate gives calcium oxide and carbon dioxide)

5.3 Displacement Reaction

A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

A + BC → AC + B
Example:
Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate)

5.4 Double Displacement Reaction

Two compounds exchange ions to form two new compounds.

AB + CD → AD + CB
Example:
AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl ↓ + NaNO₃ (Silver nitrate + sodium chloride gives silver chloride precipitate)

5.5 Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

  • Oxidation: Gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen.
  • Reduction: Loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen.
Example:
CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
CuO loses oxygen → Reduction (Cu is reduced)
H₂ gains oxygen → Oxidation (H₂ is oxidized)

6. Summary: Types of Reactions

Type Description Example
Combination A + B → AB 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Decomposition AB → A + B CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Displacement A + BC → AC + B Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
Double Displacement AB + CD → AD + CB AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
Redox Oxidation + Reduction CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O

7. Important Equations

Burning of Magnesium: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Heating of Calcium Carbonate: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Iron + Copper Sulphate: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu
Silver Nitrate + Sodium Chloride: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
Rusting of Iron: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃
